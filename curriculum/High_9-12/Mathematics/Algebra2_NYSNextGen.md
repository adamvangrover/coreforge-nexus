# High School - Algebra II - NYS Next Generation Mathematics Learning Standards

## I. Overview and Alignment
*   **Subject Description:** Algebra II is typically the third course in the New York State high school mathematics sequence, building upon concepts learned in Algebra I and Geometry. This course significantly expands students' repertoire of functions to include polynomial, rational, radical, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions. Students will deepen their understanding of algebraic manipulation, function properties, graphing techniques, and mathematical modeling. Key topics include operations with complex numbers, solving a variety of advanced equations (polynomial, rational, radical, exponential, logarithmic), understanding and applying properties of logarithms, exploring trigonometric functions via the unit circle and their graphs, and delving further into statistical analysis and probability. Successful completion of this course prepares students for the Algebra II Regents exam and provides a strong foundation for precalculus and other advanced mathematics courses.
*   **NYS Learning Standards Addressed:** This course is aligned with the **NYS Next Generation Mathematics Learning Standards for Algebra II**. The primary domains and clusters include:
    *   **Number and Quantity (N-CN, N-RN):**
        *   N-CN.A: Perform arithmetic operations with complex numbers.
        *   N-CN.C: Use complex numbers in polynomial identities and equations.
        *   N-RN.A: Extend the properties of exponents to rational exponents.
    *   **Algebra (A-SSE, A-APR, A-CED, A-REI):**
        *   A-SSE.A: Interpret the structure of expressions.
        *   A-SSE.B: Write expressions in equivalent forms to solve problems (e.g., using properties of exponents, factoring, completing the square for conics - parabolas).
        *   A-APR.B: Understand the relationship between zeros and factors of polynomials.
        *   A-APR.C: Use polynomial identities to solve problems.
        *   A-APR.D: Rewrite rational expressions.
        *   A-CED.A: Create equations that describe numbers or relationships.
        *   A-REI.A: Understand solving equations as a process of reasoning and explain the reasoning.
        *   A-REI.B: Solve equations and inequalities in one variable (including radical and rational equations).
        *   A-REI.D: Represent and solve equations and inequalities graphically (focus on systems involving non-linear functions).
    *   **Functions (F-IF, F-BF, F-LE, F-TF):**
        *   F-IF.B: Interpret functions that arise in applications in terms of the context (polynomial, rational, radical, exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric).
        *   F-IF.C: Analyze functions using different representations (graphs, tables, equations, verbal descriptions).
        *   F-BF.A: Build a function that models a relationship between two quantities.
        *   F-BF.B: Build new functions from existing functions (inverse functions, compositions, transformations).
        *   F-LE.A: Construct and compare linear, quadratic, and exponential models and solve problems (extending to logarithmic functions).
        *   F-TF.A: Extend the domain of trigonometric functions using the unit circle.
        *   F-TF.B: Model periodic phenomena with trigonometric functions.
        *   F-TF.C: Prove and apply trigonometric identities (Pythagorean identity).
    *   **Statistics and Probability (S-ID, S-IC, S-CP):**
        *   S-ID.A: Summarize, represent, and interpret data on a single count or measurement variable (focus on normal distribution).
        *   S-ID.B: Summarize, represent, and interpret data on two categorical and quantitative variables (revisiting linear regression).
        *   S-IC.A: Understand and evaluate random processes underlying statistical experiments.
        *   S-IC.B: Make inferences and justify conclusions from sample surveys, experiments, and observational studies.
        *   S-CP.A: Understand independence and conditional probability and use them to interpret data.
        *   S-CP.B: Use the rules of probability to compute probabilities of compound events in a uniform probability model.
    *   Instruction will also integrate the **Standards for Mathematical Practice** throughout the course.
*   **Federal Mandate Considerations (if applicable):**
    *   **ESSA (Every Student Succeeds Act):** Algebra II is a key component of a "well-rounded education," vital for preparing students for STEM majors in college and for careers requiring advanced analytical and problem-solving skills.
    *   **IDEA (Individuals with Disabilities Education Act):** Instruction will incorporate UDL principles. Accommodations, modifications, and supports (e.g., graphing technology, formula sheets, scaffolded notes, extended time) will be provided as per IEPs and 504 plans.
*   **Statutory Instructional Mandates Integrated (if applicable):** Not directly applicable for specific math content.

## II. Core Concepts / Units
(Logically sequenced units or major topics, aligned with NYS Next Gen Algebra II standards)
*   **Unit 1: Polynomial, Rational, and Radical Relationships** (Approx. 7-8 weeks)
    *   **Key Concepts:** Review and extension of polynomial operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division using long division and synthetic division). Factoring techniques (GCF, difference of squares, sum/difference of cubes, factoring by grouping, trinomials with a>1). The Remainder Theorem and Factor Theorem. Solving polynomial equations by factoring and finding zeros. Introduction to complex numbers (i, standard form a+bi), operations with complex numbers (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division, conjugates). Solving quadratic equations with complex solutions. Simplifying, adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing rational expressions. Solving rational equations (checking for extraneous solutions). Simplifying radical expressions (including rational exponents). Solving radical equations (checking for extraneous solutions). (N-RN.A.1-2; N-CN.A.1-2, N-CN.C.7; A-SSE.A.1-2; A-APR.B.2-3, A-APR.D.6; A-REI.A.2, A-REI.B.4b)
    *   **Learning Objectives (Proposed):**
        *   Students will be able to perform arithmetic operations on polynomials and rational expressions.
        *   Students will be able to solve polynomial, rational, and radical equations, identifying extraneous solutions where applicable.
        *   Students will be able to perform arithmetic operations with complex numbers and find complex solutions to quadratic equations.
    *   **Essential Questions:**
        *   How do the properties of real numbers extend to operations with polynomials, rational expressions, and complex numbers?
        *   What is the relationship between the factors, zeros (real and complex), and graph of a polynomial function?
        *   Why is it crucial to check for extraneous solutions when solving rational and radical equations?
*   **Unit 2: Advanced Function Analysis: Polynomial, Rational, Radical, and Piecewise Functions** (Approx. 6-7 weeks)
    *   **Key Concepts:** In-depth analysis of polynomial functions: graphing, identifying zeros, end behavior, multiplicity of roots, turning points. Graphing rational functions: identifying domain, range, vertical/horizontal/slant asymptotes, holes. Graphing radical functions (square root, cube root) and identifying domain/range. Graphing absolute value and piecewise-defined functions. Transformations of functions (translations, reflections, stretches/compressions) applied to all parent functions studied. Finding inverse functions algebraically and graphically, restricting domains to ensure invertibility. Function composition. Solving systems of equations involving linear, quadratic, and other non-linear functions (graphically and algebraically). (A-APR.B.3; A-CED.A.1-3; A-REI.D.11; F-IF.B.4, F-IF.C.7b-d, F-IF.C.8, F-IF.C.9; F-BF.A.1b, F-BF.B.3, F-BF.B.4a-c)
    *   **Learning Objectives (Proposed):**
        *   Students will be able to analyze and graph polynomial, rational, radical, and piecewise functions, identifying key features such as zeros, asymptotes, end behavior, domain, and range.
        *   Students will be able to apply transformations to graphs of functions and write equations for transformed functions.
        *   Students will be able to find and verify inverse functions and compose functions.
    *   **Essential Questions:**
        *   How do the algebraic properties of a function (e.g., degree of polynomial, factors of rational function) relate to the geometric features of its graph?
        *   How can transformations be used to efficiently graph related functions and understand their behavior?
        *   What conditions are necessary for a function to have an inverse, and how are a function and its inverse related?
*   **Unit 3: Exponential and Logarithmic Functions** (Approx. 5-6 weeks)
    *   **Key Concepts:** Review of exponential functions (growth/decay, properties of exponents). Logarithmic functions as inverses of exponential functions (log base b of x = y <=> b^y = x). Common logarithms (base 10) and natural logarithms (base e). Properties of logarithms (product, quotient, power, change of base). Solving exponential equations using logarithms. Solving logarithmic equations (checking for extraneous solutions). Graphing exponential and logarithmic functions, identifying key features (asymptotes, domain, range, intercepts). Modeling real-world phenomena with exponential and logarithmic functions (e.g., compound interest, population growth/decay, pH scale, Richter scale). (A-SSE.B.3c; F-IF.C.7e, F-IF.C.8b; F-BF.B.4a, F-BF.B.5; F-LE.A.4)
    *   **Learning Objectives (Proposed):**
        *   Students will be able to understand the relationship between exponential and logarithmic functions and convert between their forms.
        *   Students will be able to apply properties of logarithms to simplify expressions and solve exponential and logarithmic equations.
        *   Students will be able to model and solve real-world problems using exponential and logarithmic functions.
    *   **Essential Questions:**
        *   How do logarithmic functions "undo" exponential functions, and why is this inverse relationship useful?
        *   In what types of real-world situations are exponential or logarithmic models most appropriate?
        *   How can the properties of logarithms simplify complex calculations and equation solving?
*   **Unit 4: Trigonometric Functions and Applications** (Approx. 7-8 weeks)
    *   Key Concepts: Radian measure and converting between degrees and radians. The unit circle: defining sine, cosine, and tangent in terms of coordinates on the unit circle for any real number angle. Reciprocal trigonometric functions (cosecant, secant, cotangent). Graphing sine, cosine, and tangent functions: identifying amplitude, period, phase shift, vertical shift, and midline. Modeling periodic phenomena with trigonometric functions (e.g., tides, sound waves, Ferris wheels). Proving and applying the Pythagorean identity (sin^2(θ) + cos^2(θ) = 1). Solving simple trigonometric equations over a given interval. (F-IF.C.7e; F-TF.A.1-4, F-TF.B.5, F-TF.C.8)
*   **Unit 5: Statistics and Probability** (Approx. 6-7 weeks)
    *   Key Concepts: Review of measures of central tendency and dispersion. The Normal Distribution: properties, z-scores, using the empirical rule (68-95-99.7 rule) and technology to find probabilities. Statistical inference: understanding samples vs. populations. Using sample data to estimate population parameters (mean, proportion). Margin of error and confidence intervals (conceptual understanding, calculations using formulas or simulations). Evaluating claims based on data. Conditional probability and independence (P(A and B) = P(A)P(B|A), P(A and B) = P(A)P(B) for independent events). Two-way frequency tables and their use in calculating conditional probabilities. The Addition Rule (P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A and B)). Using probability to make fair decisions and analyze risks. (S-ID.A.4; S-IC.A.1-2, S-IC.B.3-6; S-CP.A.1-5, S-CP.B.6-7)
*   **Unit 6: Sequences and Series (Optional/Integrated)** (Approx. 2-3 weeks, if covered as a separate unit)
    *   Key Concepts: Defining sequences explicitly and recursively. Arithmetic sequences and series: formulas for nth term and sum of first n terms. Geometric sequences and series: formulas for nth term and sum of first n terms, sum of an infinite geometric series (if it converges). Sigma notation for series. Applications of sequences and series (e.g., compound interest, amortization - introductory). (F-IF.A.3; F-BF.A.1a, F-BF.A.2; A-SSE.B.4 - focus on geometric series)

## III. Instructional Strategies & Resources
*   **Key Pedagogical Approaches:**
    *   **UDL Principles:** Providing multiple representations of functions (algebraic, graphical, tabular, verbal), flexible options for student expression (solving, graphing, writing explanations, presenting), and varied engagement strategies (real-world problems, technology exploration, collaborative tasks).
    *   **Guided Inquiry and Investigations:** Using graphing technology (Desmos, TI-84) for students to explore function behavior, transformations, and relationships before formalizing concepts.
    *   **Collaborative Problem-Solving:** Students working in groups on complex modeling problems, multi-step equation solving, or data analysis tasks.
    *   **Modeling Real-World Data:** Using authentic datasets to apply functions (e.g., exponential decay of substances, trigonometric modeling of periodic events, statistical analysis of survey data).
    *   **Direct Instruction with Applications:** Clearly explaining advanced algebraic techniques and function properties, immediately followed by application to contextual problems.
    *   **Projects Connecting Multiple Concepts:** Culminating projects that require students to synthesize knowledge from different units (e.g., designing a roller coaster using polynomial and trigonometric functions, analyzing a financial plan using exponential functions and series).
*   **Potential Interdisciplinary Connections:**
    *   **Physics:** Modeling projectile motion (quadratics/polynomials), wave phenomena (trigonometry), radioactive decay (exponential functions).
    *   **Chemistry:** pH scale (logarithms), reaction rates.
    *   **Biology/Environmental Science:** Population growth/decay models (exponential/logarithmic functions), predator-prey cycles (trigonometric or systems).
    *   **Economics/Business/Finance:** Compound interest, loan amortization (exponential functions, series), supply/demand analysis (systems of equations), optimization problems.
    *   **Computer Science:** Algorithm complexity (logarithmic, exponential growth), transformations in graphics.
    *   **Social Sciences (Psychology/Sociology):** Statistical analysis of survey data, modeling trends.
*   **Ideas for Supplemental Materials & Technology Integration:**
    *   **Graphing Calculators:** TI-84 family (essential for Regents), Desmos (online and app, excellent for visualization and exploration).
    *   **Statistical Software or Applets:** Online tools for normal distribution calculations, confidence interval simulations, regression analysis (e.g., StatKey, applets from textbook publishers).
    *   **Spreadsheet Software:** Google Sheets, Microsoft Excel for data organization, regression, and financial modeling.
    *   **Online Function Graphers and CAS (Computer Algebra Systems):** WolframAlpha, Symbolab for checking work and exploring complex expressions (used judiciously).
    *   **Real-World Datasets:** From sources like Gapminder, government statistics (BLS, Census), scientific studies.
    *   **NYS Regents Exam Archives:** For practice and familiarity with question types.

## IV. Assessment Strategies
*   **Formative Assessments:**
    *   Regular homework assignments focusing on algebraic manipulation, function analysis, and problem-solving.
    *   Quizzes on specific function types, solving techniques (e.g., logarithmic equations, factoring polynomials), or statistical concepts.
    *   Problem sets requiring application of multiple concepts within a unit.
    *   Exit tickets to quickly assess understanding of a lesson's key objective.
    *   Class presentations of solutions or project components, allowing for peer and teacher feedback.
    *   Analysis of student work during group activities or collaborative problem-solving.
*   **Summative Assessments:**
    *   Unit tests comprising a mix of question types: algebraic manipulation, graphing and interpretation, modeling real-world scenarios, and multi-step problem-solving (Regents-style).
    *   Projects requiring students to apply multiple function types to model a complex situation, analyze data statistically, or explore a real-world application of course concepts.
    *   Performance tasks that assess students' ability to synthesize knowledge and communicate their mathematical reasoning.
    *   Cumulative mid-term and final examinations, mirroring the format and rigor of the Algebra II Regents exam.
*   **Accommodations & Modifications (IDEA/504):**
    *   Provide and support the proficient use of graphing calculators.
    *   Offer formula sheets with key identities, function properties, and statistical formulas (as allowable).
    *   Use step-by-step guides or checklists for complex algebraic procedures (e.g., long division of polynomials, solving systems with matrices if covered).
    *   Provide pre-filled notes or graphic organizers for analyzing functions or organizing statistical data.
    *   Extended time for assignments and assessments as per IEP/504.
    *   Alternative settings for tests to reduce distractions.
    *   Break down complex multi-step problems into smaller, more manageable parts with intermediate check-ins.
    *   Access to worked examples or partially completed solutions as models.
*   **ELL Support Strategies:**
    *   Develop and consistently use visual aids (graphs, diagrams, anchor charts) to represent abstract function behavior and algebraic processes.
    *   Create bilingual glossaries for advanced mathematical terminology specific to Algebra II (e.g., "asymptote," "logarithm," "radian," "polynomial").
    *   Use sentence frames and starters to help students articulate their mathematical reasoning, interpret graphs, and explain solutions to word problems.
    *   Leverage technology (Desmos, GeoGebra) to provide dynamic visual representations of functions and transformations, making abstract concepts more concrete.
    *   Pair ELLs with supportive bilingual or proficient English-speaking peers for collaborative tasks and problem-solving.
    *   Explicitly teach common mathematical language structures and how to interpret complex word problems by breaking them down.
    *   Connect abstract concepts to concrete, relatable examples or real-world scenarios whenever possible.
